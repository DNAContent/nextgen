06 / The Science

From a folk remedy to a
patented candidate.

People across the Middle East have used the black calla lily (Arum palaestinum) as medicine for centuries. Genzada isolated and synthesized compounds from the plant to create its lead candidate, GZ17-6.02.

That candidate is now in human trials, studied across multiple tumor types with academic and contract research partners. The supplement side, Hyatt, grows the same plant in dedicated greenhouses, which means the company controls its material from soil to product.

GZ17-6.02 is investigational and has not been approved by the FDA for any use. Statements about the drug program are forward-looking. Hyatt supplements are not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease.

08 / The Revenue Engine

Hyatt: science
behind the tradition.

People across the Middle East have used Arum palaestinum for more than 1,200 years. Hyatt Life Sciences is the first to commercialize it as a modern supplement, and it sells today.

This is where the science meets a paying customer. The line brings in revenue now, carries the company between clinical milestones, and feeds back real demand for the same plant the drug program is built on.

Selling the black calla lily in the U.S. food supply meant going to the FDA directly. NextGen completed New Dietary Ingredient notifications for both Arum palaestinum and Peganum harmala. By the company's account, it is the only U.S. entity with both cleared.

Products grow in six greenhouses in the same Kansas town where Jacam was built, and sell through Walmart.com and the company's own store.3
